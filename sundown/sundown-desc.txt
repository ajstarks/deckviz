Sundown towns emerged in the 1800s and used geographic boundaries to exclude Black and other non-white residents through violence, threats, signage, and local enforcement. Though often absent from official records, community memory, and research, archival evidence suggest thousands of U.S. towns practiced these forms of racial exclusion. Mapping and documenting sundown towns helps reveal how historical spatial exclusion continues to shape present-day segregation, resource distribution, and access to quality health care.
